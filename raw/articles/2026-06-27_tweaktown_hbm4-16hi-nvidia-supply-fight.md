# SK hynix, Samsung, and Micron fighting for NVIDIA supply contracts for new 16-Hi HBM4 orders

Samsung, SK hynix, and Micron are accelerating development of 16-Hi HBM4 memory chips for NVIDIA, after NVIDIA reportedly requested that domestic and foreign memory manufacturers deliver 16-Hi HBM memory chips by Q4 2026. Concrete contracts have not yet been signed; discussions are underway internally regarding initial production volumes.

An industry official said: "Following the supply of 12-Hi HBM4, a request for 16-Hi supply has also been made, so we are establishing a very fast development schedule. Performance evaluation could begin as early as before the third quarter of next year."

12-Hi HBM4 will enter mass supply in early 2026, but development of denser 16-Hi HBM4 chips has accelerated. Industry sources suggest the 16-Hi product might be branded HBM4E depending on performance and mass-production timing.

16-Hi HBM stacks are the densest HBM stack count attempted so far — next-gen HBM5 is also expected to max out at 16-Hi, with 20-Hi/24-Hi not expected until HBM7 (~2035) and HBM8.

Technical challenges are "formidable," particularly around wafer thickness: existing 12-Hi HBM has a wafer thickness of about 50µm, which the report suggests must be reduced to around 30µm to stack 16 layers.

The JEDEC-defined HBM4 thickness standard is 775µm — about 50µm thicker than HBM3E's 725µm — giving leeway because thinning becomes harder as layer count increases. However, the prevailing industry opinion is that JEDEC will not further increase the thickness allowance for 16-Hi HBM, meaning more DRAM dies must be stacked within the same 775µm envelope.

Currently, Samsung and Micron use Thermal Compression Non-Conductive Film (TC-NCF) bonding, while SK hynix uses Mass Reflow Molded Underfill (MR-MUF). An industry official noted: "The current bonding material thickness is around 10µm, and to stack more DRAMs, this must be reduced to secure competitiveness. Dissipating the heat generated after bonding is also a challenge."
