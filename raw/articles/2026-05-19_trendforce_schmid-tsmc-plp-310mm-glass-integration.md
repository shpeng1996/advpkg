As AI accelerator leaders such as NVIDIA continue rolling out chips with increasingly larger compute die sizes, panel-level packaging is gaining momentum. Germany-headquartered SCHMID — an equipment provider for PCB and IC substrate manufacturing — said in an Investing.com earnings call transcript that the panel-level packaging market could expand 3–4x by 2030. The company also highlighted that its key customer TSMC is advancing 310×310 mm panel formats while evaluating glass integration at these same scale points.

SCHMID Chief Sales Officer Roland Rettenmaier added that multiple panel sizes — mainly 310×310 mm, 510×515 mm, and 600×600 mm — are increasingly being standardized across the ecosystem by customers including TSMC, Intel, and Samsung, along with their broader supply chains.

Panel-level packaging follows a "rectangular instead of circular" approach, replacing traditional silicon wafers with glass substrates to significantly reduce edge waste. The shift in materials is expected to overcome the physical limitations of organic substrates and silicon interposers, while enabling higher chip density.

TSMC has branded its panel-level packaging platform "CoPoS" (Chip-on-Panel-on-Substrate), with industry sources pointing to potential mass production as early as 2028.

## Key Supply Chain Moves

Intel is an indirect key customer through its supply chain — it does not operate in-house panel, PCB, or substrate production and instead relies on partners such as Japan's Ibiden and Unimicron. Major players — driven by Intel and other large OEMs — are now beginning to line up, starting with small-scale production before gradually scaling up.

Samsung and its supply chain — particularly Samsung Electro-Mechanics (SEMCO) — are also progressing glass core substrate adoption.

SKC's U.S. subsidiary Absolics (Covington, Georgia Plant 1 — the world's first mass-production line for glass substrates) is currently in trial production, with the goal of achieving timely mass ramp-up. SKC has secured multiple global tier-one customers across HPC, AI servers, and high-frequency wireless communications.
