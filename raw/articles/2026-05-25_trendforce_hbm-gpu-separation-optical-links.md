As AI workloads continue to scale, growing demand for memory capacity and bandwidth is pushing current GPU-HBM architectures toward their limits. A researcher at a major Korean memory manufacturer said memory and packaging companies are discussing a new approach that places GPUs and HBM in separate packages. Instead of positioning HBM directly beside the GPU as in conventional designs, the two would be connected through optical links. This architecture could allow GPUs to support several times more HBM than existing designs.

The industry has traditionally increased HBM capacity by stacking more memory stacks vertically. However, as HBM advances beyond 12-layer and 16-layer designs toward 20 layers and above, manufacturing complexity has risen sharply while physical constraints have become increasingly difficult to overcome.

What happens if HBM stack counts can no longer keep increasing? The alternative — expanding capacity by adding more HBM units horizontally around the GPU — is also reaching limits. In current 2.5D packaging architectures, GPUs and HBM sit closely together on a single substrate, meaning the number of HBM units that can fit within the GPU's limited perimeter ("shoreline") remains tightly constrained.

## GPU-HBM Separation Architecture

One approach gaining attention is separating GPUs and HBM into different packages. The concept challenges the traditional assumption that chips must remain adjacent to minimize latency, instead proposing physical separation while offsetting longer transmission distances through ultra-fast optical signaling.

Multiple architectural options are being explored for where HBM could be positioned:
- Making greater use of the space surrounding the GPU
- Placing HBM in isolated areas beneath the GPU board (requiring vertical motherboard extension, meaning system form factor changes)

The OSAT industry is closely monitoring this trend, particularly given the potential adoption of optical interconnects.

## Key Challenge: Miniaturizing Photonic Technology

Optical links for HBM are based on principles similar to those used in optical communications between servers within data centers. A key challenge lies in shrinking photonic technologies designed for large-scale systems down to microscopic dimensions within a single board or chipset. Implementing optical links for HBM in highly constrained board space would require photonic components to become significantly smaller and more densely integrated.
