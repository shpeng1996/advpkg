# Intel's Glass Substrate Bet Inches Closer to Reality as First Prototypes With Co-Packaged Optics Appear Ahead of 2030 Rollout

Source: Wccftech, by Hassan Mujtaba, 20 May 2026

The first Glass Core Substrate prototypes, featuring co-packaged optics, were pictured at OFC 2026 (Optical Fiber Communication Conference), giving a glimpse of future chips. Dr. Ian Cutress of More Than Moore spotted prototypes of a glass-core substrate-based chip with an AOP (Active Optical Package) — mockups showcasing what next-generation chips will look like.

Glass Core substrates have been gaining interest due to several benefits over traditional organic substrate solutions. Current organic substrates are also facing shortages due to the AI supercycle, leading major substrate supplier Ajinomoto to raise prices. These supply constraints are pushing the industry toward Glass substrates as an alternative.

In the pictured prototypes, two substrate types are shown: one ceramics-based, one glass-based (transparent, distinguishing it from purplish-brown ceramic and green organic substrates). The mockup shows four compute chiplets, four DRAM chiplets, and eight smaller chiplets, with eight yellow chips on the substrate's outskirts being the most notable feature — these are co-packaged optical interfaces.

With co-packaged optics, electrical signals are converted to light using optical transceivers on the same package, reducing reliance on copper for data transfer. Silicon photonics is expected to change how next-gen AI data centers operate, offering a scale-up in bandwidth and transfer speeds. Nvidia and AMD are both racing to deliver the first CPO solutions by 2027-2028.

Intel has already announced it is working on glass substrates to replace organic packages, and partners such as Amkor have stated readiness within three years — suggesting a first rollout around 2029-2030.

Benefits of Glass Core Substrates cited include 10x interconnect densities, the ability to cram more chiplets than organic substrates, seamless integration with optical interfaces such as CPO, and higher yields from rectangular glass panels versus traditional round wafers.
