In late June, Samsung, SK hynix, and Micron were sued in the U.S. by 14 consumers and three small PC assembly and distribution firms over allegations of coordinated supply cuts that pushed memory prices up nearly 700% in four years, drawing close industry attention to how the case will unfold.

Citing legal experts, the case could unfold in two directions: parallel price hikes alone rarely prove collusion — plaintiffs must show concrete evidence that competitors coordinated behind the scenes, whether through sharing sensitive pricing/production data or using industry meetings to align strategies.

## Key Facts of the Case

- Defendants: Samsung, SK hynix, Micron (collectively ~90% global DRAM market)
- Plaintiffs: 14 consumers + 3 small PC assembly/distribution firms
- Allegation: Coordinated reduction of legacy commodity DRAM (DDR3/DDR4) production while using HBM expansion as justification, driving prices up ~700% in four years
- Defendants have not yet responded in court; expected to file motions to dismiss

## HBM Expansion as Evidence

The latest lawsuit centers on allegations that the three makers coordinated to reduce production of legacy commodity DRAM while using HBM expansion as justification. Like village bakeries shifting ovens toward premium wedding cakes (HBM), leaving basic bread (commodity DRAM) in short supply and pushing prices higher.

Key difference from previous DRAM legal cases: plaintiffs' focus on the HBM transition and whether it can serve as direct evidence of coordinated supply reductions.

A semiconductor attorney noted that a simple timing overlap between HBM expansion and DRAM production cuts may not be sufficient to prove collusion.

## Company Responses

- **Samsung**: Dismissed claims as unfounded; business conducted in full compliance with fair competition principles
- **SK hynix**: Currently reviewing the lawsuit; will take appropriate action after assessment

## Company Defense Arguments

- Surging AI server demand provides legitimate explanation for rising memory prices
- Recent capacity expansion plans (Micron Hiroshima, SK hynix Yongin, Samsung) contradict intentional supply restriction narrative
- Companies intentionally restricting supply would have little reason to invest heavily in expanding production

## Historical Precedent

- 1998-2002: DRAM suppliers found to have fixed memory prices sold to major PC makers (Dell, HP, Compaq, IBM, etc.)
- 2005: Samsung received $300M criminal antitrust fine (second-largest in U.S. history at the time); Hynix $185M; Infineon $160M; Elpida $84M
- 2018 class-action: Alleged Samsung, SK hynix, Micron coordinated to curb DRAM supply during 2016-2018; dismissed in 2022 — insufficient evidence of agreement or coordinated effort

## Implication if Lawsuit Survives

If the lawsuit survives motions to dismiss, Samsung, SK hynix, and Micron could be compelled to disclose internal communications related to HBM allocation and commodity DRAM reductions to plaintiffs' attorneys for the first time.
