## EMIB Challenges Test Intel amid Buzz of NVIDIA Considering 14A/18A and Advanced Packaging

*TrendForce, 2026-01-29*

As NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang arrived in Taipei and reportedly reaffirmed cooperation with Intel on x86 CPU development, market attention has refocused on how Team Green will navigate the rising wave of dual-foundry strategies. The renewed interest comes as reports suggest major tech players, including NVIDIA and Apple, are evaluating Intel's 14A and 18A nodes, along with its advanced packaging.

Big tech firms such as NVIDIA are considering outsourcing less complex, lower-risk chips to Intel—such as Feynman's I/O die on 14A or 18A—to protect volume ramp-up in case Intel falls short on yield or capacity, while keeping GPU core production anchored at TSMC. South Korea's EBN reported that Intel may be eyeing production at its Arizona plant.

NVIDIA may also be exploring outsourcing other non-core products to Intel, potentially bringing next-generation gaming GPUs into a foundry deal with Team Blue.

Apple is also evaluating Intel's 18A (18A-P) and 14A, though questions remain over whether Intel can provide sufficient advanced capacity for external customers by 2028. Apple's base M-series chips—used in cost-sensitive Macs and tablets—are prime candidates due to smaller die sizes, cheaper packaging, and greater tolerance for yield and performance variation.

While market chatter suggests NVIDIA is considering Intel's EMIB to package Feynman, Tom's Hardware highlights significant technical hurdles. Feynman chips are expected to draw 5–6 kW—far beyond what traditional board-level voltage regulation can handle. To manage these extreme currents, the processors will rely on integrated voltage regulators (IVR) embedded within the package itself.

IVR allows power to enter the package at a higher voltage (~1.8V), reducing current per bump, improving efficiency, cutting voltage ripple, and enabling 10–100× faster response than on-board VRMs.

However, while Intel's EMIB can accommodate co-packaged IVR components, it is not true embedded IVR and lacks the capability to support 5–6 kW AI accelerators. Intel's Foveros technologies—such as Foveros Omni and Direct3D—can deliver multi-kilowatt power with low IR drop by stacking dedicated power tiles with logic, but adopting this approach would require a complete redesign of Feynman.

As a result, while packaging Feynman in the U.S. may sound appealing, NVIDIA is more likely to wait for TSMC to roll out advanced packaging in the U.S. later this decade, given the hurdles EMIB and Foveros face with high-end GPUs. That said, Intel could still land packaging work for Vera CPUs or offer custom Xeon processors to NVIDIA data centers.
