# Researchers Reportedly Develop Ultrafast Laser Wiring for Glass Substrates, Targeting CPO Bottleneck

Published: 2026-04-30 | Source: TrendForce (citing ETNews)

## Key Development: ULCVD Technology

A research team led by Professor Han Seung-hoe from the Department of Mechanical Engineering at Chonnam National University (South Korea) has developed "ultrashort-pulse laser-induced chemical vapor deposition (ULCVD)" technology.

The method uses an ultrafast femtosecond laser to directly inscribe conductive carbon circuits on both sides of transparent substrates without the need for masks, enabling flexible and selective wiring across the entire surface.

The breakthrough is expected to drive advances in:
- 3D glass-based optical-electrical convergence semiconductors for co-packaged optics (CPO)
- High-precision quantum sensors

## Problem Being Solved

Forming three-dimensional interconnects in processes such as through-glass vias (TGV) and redistribution layers (RDL)—linking the top and bottom surfaces of glass substrates—remains a significant technical challenge. This is a key bottleneck in glass substrate commercialization for CPO and advanced packaging.

## Technical Details

- Femtosecond lasers can pass through transparent glass, leveraging nonlinear absorption effects
- Enables flexible and selective wiring on both front and back surfaces simultaneously
- Achieves electrical conductivity comparable to best laser-induced graphene (LIG) wiring in academia
- Confirmed potential to form interconnects inside through-holes and on complex 3D curved structures

## Future Roadmap

Prof. Han plans to expand the ULCVD process beyond carbon-based wiring to include metal materials such as copper (Cu) and gold (Au)—key materials for future semiconductor packaging.

## Industry Context

Commercialization of glass substrates is accelerating:
- Intel: roadmap targeting 2030 for glass substrate advantage
- Absolics (SKC subsidiary): building world's first glass substrate mass-production facility in Georgia, targeting 2026 production ahead of Intel
